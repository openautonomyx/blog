From Institutions To Individuals - A Founder's Perspective

Created on 2026-01-15 13:33

Published on 2026-01-15 13:42

There’s a frustration I’ve carried for a long time.

If I post something on one platform, people on another platform are left out. Posting it again feels repetitive, inefficient, and frankly unnecessary.

What bothers me is not the inconvenience. What bothers me is that this problem has already been solved—just not for individuals.

Companies automated this years ago.

So I started asking myself: why are individuals still excluded?

This Isn’t a Technology Limitation

Technology does not know whether you are an individual or a business.

It doesn’t care.

All it needs is:

  • Authentication

  • Authorization

  • An API key

Automation, workflows, analytics, scheduling, and cross-platform publishing are all solved problems. Platforms already expose the required APIs. The infrastructure already exists.

Yet we still have:

  • Personal accounts

  • Business accounts

  • Separate logins

  • Separate dashboards

  • Separate rules

This separation is not technical. It is a product and business decision.

“Different Use Cases” Is an Incomplete Argument

The most common justification is that individuals and businesses have different use cases.

That’s true—but it’s not a reason to fragment platforms.

Enterprise software solved this problem decades ago using:

  • Granular role-based access control

  • Permissioning

  • Feature flags

  • Usage-based billing

If a system like Oracle NetSuite can manage complex global enterprises with fine-grained access controls, then modern social and SaaS platforms can absolutely do the same for individuals who also operate as businesses.

The separation persists not because it must—but because it has always been that way.

The World Has Changed. The Tools Haven’t.

The way people work has fundamentally shifted.

  • The gig economy is growing

  • Individuals are primary content creators

  • Solo YouTube creators earn more than many organizations

  • Governments recognize One Person Companies

  • Individuals can now protect liability and operate legally as businesses

An individual today can be:

  • A creator

  • A consultant

  • A founder

  • A company of one

  • A global operator

Legally, economically, and operationally.

The only thing lagging behind is the tooling.

Do Individuals Have the Tools to Operate Like Businesses?

They should.

And increasingly, they need to.

The question is no longer “Should individuals have access to business tools?” The real question is “Why are we still designing systems that assume they shouldn’t?”

Why We Built OpenSaaS This Way

At OpenSaaS, we do not differentiate between individuals and businesses.

Not because they are the same—but because the distinction is irrelevant at the product level.

At the end of the day:

  • You sell to humans

  • You support humans

  • You solve problems for humans

A freelancer’s time is as valuable as a CEO’s. A solo founder’s need is as real as an enterprise team’s.

We treat them that way.

Our Approach

We don’t create separate platforms for “individuals” and “businesses.”

Instead:

  • We build applications, each designed around a specific use case

  • Every user gets the same feature set

  • The same documentation

  • The same training

  • The same quality bar

Pricing is based on usage, not the number of users.

User-based licensing made sense in a pre-AI world. It makes far less sense now.

Looking Ahead

What works well today won’t necessarily work well tomorrow.

The independent worker is no longer an exception—it’s becoming the norm. Tools must evolve to reflect this reality.

Over the coming days, I’ll share how we think about this in practice—through product decisions, architecture, and real-world use cases.

This isn’t about being anti-platform. It’s about being honest about how work actually happens now.

And building accordingly.

— Founder, OpenSaaS
