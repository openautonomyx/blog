Making Software Truly Usable: The OpenSaaS Vision

Created on 2026-01-12 00:32

Published on 2026-01-12 00:43

Good morning, everyone.

When I first set out to make technology accessible, I assumed that access was the main barrier. But over time, I realized something important: technology has been accessible for a long time.

Cloud made it accessible. Open-source software has existed for decades.

So why do we still see slow adoption—or no adoption at all—in so many cases?

When I thought about it more deeply, I realized the real problem isn’t accessibility. The real problem is usability—specifically, enterprise-grade usability and trust.

If I’m adopting software for a critical business process, I need to be absolutely confident that it will work reliably and securely. That confidence doesn’t come from a demo or a feature list. It comes from evaluation, readiness, and operational clarity.

And this is exactly where adoption breaks down—especially in small and mid-sized companies.

There is always a resource crunch.

Teams need to deal with:

  • security approvals

  • data access and stakeholder alignment

  • internal testing

  • audits and compliance

  • procurement workflows

  • ongoing governance

So adoption is no longer just about choosing the right tool. The additional work around the tool becomes the real cost—and the real bottleneck.

In large enterprises, this problem is solved structurally. There are entire functions dedicated to evaluation and risk reduction. External authorities like Gartner often become de facto benchmarks that guide decisions and reduce uncertainty.

But in the SMB ecosystem, there is a clear vacuum.

That’s where OpenSaaS comes in.

OpenSaaS exists to help businesses adopt software quickly, confidently, and at low cost. We provide the missing support layer that simplifies evaluation, reduces risk, accelerates approvals, and makes adoption practical—benefiting both software sellers and users.

How This Thinking Translates to Open Source (Linux Foundation Example)

To give you a concrete example of how this philosophy plays out, I want to share a snippet of my thinking from the Linux Foundation community.

"

I don’t have a long history in open source—but I deeply understand its importance.

That’s why I’m currently working full-time on a mission to bring open-source solutions to small businesses, solo founders, freelancers, and power users. To support this vision, I’ve registered my company, OpenSaaS LLC, and I’m committed to pursuing this mission wholeheartedly.

Once again, accessibility is not the real problem in open source. We already know where the code lives.

Usability is the real barrier to mass adoption.

Small things make a massive difference.

For example, having a simple “Register User” page instead of requiring users to configure usernames and passwords inside a backend Docker Compose file can be the difference between:

  • someone successfully adopting an open-source product and becoming its advocate, or

  • abandoning it entirely because they couldn’t even sign in after self-hosting

The lack of clear:

  • how-to guides

  • onboarding flows

  • operational best practices

continues to block adoption—even when the underlying software is excellent."

This is exactly the gap I want to solve."

The Bigger Goal

My vision is simple:

On one side, improve software quality and readiness. On the other, increase awareness, confidence, and adoption.

In other words: bring the “WordPress moment” to open source and SaaS adoption—where powerful software becomes usable, approachable, and trusted by anyone, not just experts.

If we get this right, we can build a better digital ecosystem—one where innovation isn’t limited by usability, fear, or hidden operational costs.
